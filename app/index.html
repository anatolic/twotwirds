The best from nature

Given all the negative consequences, business as usual will not be feasible anymore in the future. While conventional cotton covers around 2,8 % of global arable land, it accounts for 6,3 % by value of global pesticide sales, including 12,3 % (!) of global insecticide sales. This is more than any other major global crop uses, with partly devastating effects for smallholder farmers, local communities, and the surrounding environment.

Luckily, there are alternatives. At Twothirds, we are using a variety of fabrics to omit the use of conventional cotton, namely Tencel™, hemp, linen (or by it’s crop name: flax), and obviously organic cotton. All of those alternatives have different characteristics, which is why we are using them for different purposes. But they have something in common: they use less resources than conventional cotton and have less negative environmental impacts, which in turn is beneficial for everyone involved: soils, rivers and waters, air, as well as local communities and farmers.

Quality

Within 15 years (from 2000 to 2014) worldwide clothing production doubled (!). At the same time, people buy 60% more clothes and keep them for half as long as 15 years ago. The phenomenon of fast fashion comes with some spectacular numbers. And spectacular social and environmental problems.

We think, the solution towards more sustainable fashion lies not simply in using different fabrics, but also comes with longevity.

And longevity is created through timelessness and quality. Timelessness obviously is a design issue. Quality - on the other hand - is created through a careful selection of all steps when producing clothes: the quality of the raw material, the production processes, the know-how of our suppliers. In order to deliver the highest quality, we keep production as local and centralized as possible.

We closely work with the same suppliers in Portugal, France, Italy, and Spain for years, which results in relationships that are built on trust and security. By keeping the distances short, the connection to our suppliers is more direct and enhanced by frequent visits. Therefore, we know how our suppliers work and they know how we think. Apart from that, we pay attention to the details. Trimmings, such as zippers and buttons, are a major reason for products to be thrown away. So by using high quality trimmings, we make your products last longer!

Recycling and Reusing

There is a variety of ways to achieve sustainable fashion, like for example the use of more sustainable materials and production processes, sufficiency, as well as responsible production and consumption. Another important pillar is the recirculation of materials and resources. Currently, one garbage truck of textiles is landfilled or incinerated every single second(!). We think that this is a loss of resources. Consequently, we started to use fabrics that include materials from different sources: they can be leftovers from production processes, post consumer waste, materials that have been recovered from the open environment (such as the ocean), and deadstock (fabrics that already have been produced but that are not used by the producer anymore).

We think, the solution towards more sustainable fashion lies not simply in using different fabrics, but also comes with longevity.

  • Reduced water usage in production processes

  • Quality deadstock fabrics

  • Less plastic

  • Recycled fishing net and other waste materials from the environment

Those different sources lead to the creation of a variety of fabrics and blends (such as recycled Nylon, recycled cotton, recycled Polyester) that meet our quality criteria. Thereby, resources are protected and the CO2 footprint is reduced (for example, for the production of Econyl, a recycled Nylon, around 60% less greenhouse gases are emitted compared to the production of virgin Nylon). Furthermore, our recycled fabrics require less land and consume less water. That‘s a lot of less, isn‘t it? And, on top of it, we are proudly supporting innovative technologies that actually make a difference!

The production of TWOTHIRDS clothing is divided in three production steps: the raw material phase, the fabric production phase, and the clothes production phase.

RAW MATERIALS PHASE

The raw materials come from several parts around the globe, including the EU, China, or Argentina. The second and third phase exclusively take place in the EU.

FABRIC PRODUCTION PHASE

Our fabrics are produced in different countries: Austria, France, Italy, Portugal.

CLOTHES PRODUCTION PHASE

The final manufacturing stages (that is, cutting and sewing) all take place in Portugal.

Our production phase in Portugal is not without a reason: the Northern region of Portugal has a historically grown textile manufacturing industry, known for its high quality and precise craftsmanship.

Additionally, frequent on-site visits enable us to create long-term and trustworthy relationships. We strongly believe that security and fair payments create an environment of trust, which benefits the product and all actors involved. By keeping the final production stage local and centralized, we can achieve exactly this, which makes us not only proud, but also convinced about the superior quality of Twothirds clothing.

Also, we are very happy, that the production facilities, where the so-called wet processes take place (such as dying and washing) have on-site wastewater treatment plants, which makes the production, which contributes to increasing water quality in Northern Portugal. You can see, by producing in Portugal we can not only ensure production that is made in the EU, but production that is actually better!

Manufacturing in Europe means security standards in production facilities leading to improved work health and no unpaid extra hours or even child labor!

Wages and work safety

Payments and safety are two huge issues in the fashion industry, just remember all the horrible stories from collapsing production sites in Bangladesh. Here, production in Portugal, Italy, and France helps, since the production facilities are regulated under European standards.

We can be sure that the paid wages are not only a randomly calculated minimum wage, which equals to the lowest wage workers can be paid. The minimum wage is determined by governments. However, the minimum wage does not necessarily correspond with the living wage, which is the wage a single person needs in order to have a life without monetary problems while all needs are covered). In Portugal and France (in Italy, there is no official minimum wage),the minimum wage is at least as high as the living wage, which is by far not the case in Bangladesh or India! For more information on minimum and living wages, check wageindicator.org

Pre-order system

Textile overproduction is a huge problem in the fashion industry. It is unknown, how much clothing is produced and not sold every year. Some estimate that in some big retail chains, about 10% of the garments are not sold. This leads to enormous amounts of clothes lying around. Naturally, this implies wasted fabric, dyes, environmental impacts and so on. Basically, it‘s burdening the environment without benefitting anyone. More cynically, some brands have been reported to burn excess production to make space for new collections. We think that this is irresponsible behavior and disrespectful not only to the people who can‘t afford clothes, but also to the environment. It‘s basically a sign of lack of appreciation. We go into a different direction: we prevent this to happen.

You might have seen that you can pre-order Twothirds clothes in our online store. The reason for this is as simple as sustainable: by offering the pre-order, we can estimate how much pieces of a specific garment we are going to sell, and subsequently order specific quantities. Use what we have

At Twothirds, we do not only produce what is ordered, we also use what we have. That means, we use up the things that we produce and order ourselves. Consequently, no deadstock arises from our production. If you don‘t know what deadstock is, here you go: deadstock is an amount of product that a company has bought or made but is unable to sell. For example, fabrics that are ordered by companies but not used to produce garments, become dead stock. There are brands out there who use dead stock to produce own garments (including ourselves), but at the same time, we think that it is the best option to not even produce dead stock.

Quality deadstock fabrics

At Twothirds, our policy includes using up fabrics and other materials that we have ordered until nothing is left, since we want to produce as little as possible.

For example, if we order a fabric, and after the season, we have some of it left, we will use the same fabric for the next season again for a different piece. The same applies to trimmings and all kinds of smaller items that are used in our clothing. First, we use what we have, and then we can think of something new. This makes our production sometimes a little more challenging than it normally would be, but at the same time we‘re being creative with what‘s already there. And we think that this is a capability quite valuable in every part of our lives.

Green innovation

Are you familiar with the concept of externalities? An externality is an impact, that is caused by goods productions, the producer is not accountable for. There are two ways of handling externalities: either through payments (e.g. carbon offsetting) or through minimising them, which basically means to strategically improve production processes and the way a company works from a sustainability perspective.

While paying for externalities is a hot topic at the moment, in general, it is always recommended to prevent those externalities from occurring, instead of paying for them afterwards. Even more so, since it does not only matter that carbon is removed from the atmosphere, but also where this happens. Furthermore, carbon offsetting does not account for other externalities, such as toxic emissions. This is why at Twothirds we like to support sustainable and green innovation (so-called eco-innovation) on both levels, technical and strategic. They are essential for decreasing our environmental footprints, and to decrease externalities (not only CO2 emissions, but all kinds of externalities).

Our drawdown program

Let us introduce Twothirds drawdown. This is our program to deal with those externalities, and it works as follows: For every order, 1€ is collected and set aside. With this money, we support different projects that improve the sustainability aspects of our supply chain. In practice, this means to collaborate with various actors, such as (research) institutes, different suppliers, and other organizations to fund innovative research on textiles and production processes. It also involves to support suppliers with investments in renewable energy production, which leads to positive effects not only for Twothirds, but also for other brands working with the same facility.
